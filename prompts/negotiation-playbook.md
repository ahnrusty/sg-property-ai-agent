# Negotiation Playbook

Information leverage matters more than tactics. Do the data work first, then negotiate.

## Pre-negotiation prep

1. Pull the last 6 to 12 months of transacted prices for the same project (URA Realis) or same flat type / block (HDB Resale Statistics).
2. Compute median, 25th, and 75th percentile $/psf for comparable units (same size band, same age band).
3. Identify the listing's percentile within that distribution. Above the 75th percentile means premium pricing; you need a concrete justification or a price cut.
4. Check days on market: a listing that has sat > 60 days usually has motivated sellers.
5. Check seller motivation: relocating overseas, divorce, estate sale, upgrading. Motivated sellers move on price.
6. Check the agent's other listings in the same project. If they have multiple, they may steer you to the one with highest commission.

## Anchoring

- Open with a number tied to data, not a percentage off list. Example: "Last three transactions in the same stack at this size cleared at $1,650 psf. Your unit is asking $1,780. I can move at $1,680 with completion in 8 weeks."
- Never reveal your maximum budget. State a working budget below your true cap.
- Give a reason for every offer. Numbers tied to specifics (lease decay, maintenance arrears, view obstructed by upcoming GLS site) hold better than round-number requests.

## Counter-offer patterns

- Seller comes down by less than 1%: they are testing patience. Hold the line, restate your evidence, walk away politely for 24 hours.
- Seller comes down by 2 to 5%: they are negotiating in good faith. Counter once more closer to your target.
- Seller refuses to budge: ask what would need to be true for them to consider your number. Listen for non-price levers (longer completion, leave-back lease, fixtures included).

## Non-price levers

Sometimes a deal closes by trading non-price terms instead of price:

- Longer completion timeline (12 to 16 weeks) for sellers who need time
- Shorter completion (4 to 6 weeks) for sellers who need cash
- Fittings and fixtures included (aircons, built-ins, curtains, light fittings)
- Leave-back lease (seller stays as tenant for 1 to 3 months post-completion)
- Furniture included with explicit list
- Renovation defect rectification before completion
- Cash component flexibility (HDB COV negotiation specifically)

## Common seller objections and counters

| Objection | Counter |
|-----------|---------|
| "I have other interested parties" | Politely ask for the latest offer they have in writing. Often they don't. |
| "I already priced it below market" | Present your data. Ask which transactions they are benchmarking against. |
| "I need this number to pay off my loan" | Acknowledge, but their loan is not your problem. Stay on market value. |
| "Reno alone cost me $XXX" | Renovation depreciates fast. Offer 30 to 40% of stated reno cost as goodwill. |
| "My agent says it will go up" | Forecasts are not facts. Stay on observable transacted prices. |

## When to walk away

- The numbers don't work even at your target offer
- A red flag emerges in due diligence (litigation, leaks, encumbrance)
- Your gut says no after the second viewing
- Your spouse / family is not aligned
- The seller refuses to provide MCST minutes, CSC, or title-related documents

Walking away is a negotiation move, not a failure. The market always has another unit next month.

## When to accept quickly

- Listed at or below the median for comparable transactions
- The exact unit and stack you wanted
- Title and physical condition clean
- Seller agreeing to your terms with minor adjustment
- Other genuine offers visible (verifiable, not agent talk)

## Drafting an offer

A clean offer letter or WhatsApp message contains:

1. Buyer name (and co-buyer if any) with residency status
2. Property address with block and unit number
3. Offer price in figures and words
4. Option period requested
5. Completion date requested
6. Inclusions (fixtures, fittings, furniture if any)
7. Conditions (subject to bank loan approval, valuation, lawyer review)
8. Deposit method and amount
9. Validity of offer (typically 24 to 48 hours)
10. Buyer's lawyer details (or "to be appointed")

## Post-acceptance

- Sign OTP and pay option fee (typically 1% for private, $1,000 for HDB resale)
- Engage lawyer immediately
- Schedule valuation (mandatory for HDB resale; lender will require for private)
- Exercise OTP within window (14 days private, 21 days HDB resale typically)
- Pay BSD and ABSD within 14 days of OTP signing
- Track completion milestones with lawyer
