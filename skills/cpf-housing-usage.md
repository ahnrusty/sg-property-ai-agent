# CPF Housing Usage

CPF Ordinary Account (OA) can be used for property purchase and mortgage servicing, but with limits. Always verify with the CPF Board before committing.

## What CPF OA can pay for

- Down payment (above the minimum cash component required by LTV rules)
- Monthly mortgage instalments
- Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD)
- ABSD on new launches (reimbursement model; not on resale ABSD)
- Legal fees (within CPF allowable limits)
- Stamp duty for legal documents
- Survey, valuation, and HDB resale application fees

CPF cannot pay for: agent commission (buyer side), renovation, furniture, monthly maintenance fees, property tax, fire insurance.

## Valuation Limit (VL) and Withdrawal Limit (WL)

- **Valuation Limit**: the lower of the purchase price or valuation at the time of purchase. CPF use is initially capped at the VL for the entire property.
- **Withdrawal Limit**: 120% of the VL. If you wish to use more CPF beyond VL, you can do so up to the WL **only if** you have set aside the prevailing Basic Retirement Sum (BRS) in your Retirement Account or OA + Special Account at age 55+.

## Lease-to-age-95 rule

To use CPF OA for the full valuation limit, the remaining lease of the property must cover the **youngest** buyer/co-owner up to at least age **95**.

If the lease falls short, CPF usage is **pro-rated** using this formula:

> Pro-rated CPF cap = VL × (remaining lease covering youngest buyer to age 95) / (full lease term needed to reach age 95)

If the remaining lease covers the youngest buyer to less than age 65, **zero CPF** can be used.

### Worked example

Youngest buyer 35 years old. Buying a 99-year leasehold condo with 50 years remaining. To cover age 95, youngest needs lease to extend 60 more years. Property only covers 50.

Pro-rated CPF cap = VL × 50/60 ≈ 83% of VL.

## Accrued interest

Every dollar of CPF OA used for property earns notional interest of **2.5% p.a. compounded** as if it had stayed in the CPF account. On sale of the property, you must return:

- Principal CPF used
- Accrued interest

If sale proceeds (after outstanding loan) are insufficient to cover principal + accrued interest, you may be in **negative sale**. The shortfall does not need to be paid in cash (waiver applies if sold at market value), but CPF is not topped up.

### Accrued interest worked example

You used $200k CPF over 10 years to service a mortgage (assume linear withdrawal for simplicity).

Approximate accrued interest at 2.5% compounded ≈ $28k to $35k depending on timing.

On sale, you return $200k principal + ~$30k accrued interest = ~$230k to your OA. This continues to earn interest in OA and can be used for the next property.

## Practical CPF planning

1. **Project CPF accrued interest** using a CPF Home Calculator (CPF Board provides one). High CPF use today reduces cash from sale tomorrow.
2. **Cash vs CPF trade-off**: paying mortgage in cash preserves CPF balance growth at 2.5% to 3.5% p.a. (OA / SA). Useful for retirement planning but reduces liquid cash.
3. **Joint borrowers**: each contributes their own CPF; on sale, each returns to their own OA based on contribution share.
4. **Refinancing**: does not reset accrued interest. Accrued interest continues to compound.

## CPF for second property

If you already own a property using CPF, additional rules apply:

- Set aside **Basic Retirement Sum (BRS)** in OA + SA + RA before using CPF for second property
- Otherwise, second property must be paid in cash for amounts beyond the BRS shortfall
- BRS is reviewed annually; current BRS (2026) is approximately $107k (verify)

## Common CPF gotchas

- Buying older leasehold property with short lease → CPF heavily pro-rated or zero
- Selling at a loss → negative sale; CPF principal lost, accrued interest waived (not refunded)
- Decoupling for second property → CPF refund mechanics, IRAS / IRAS valuation requirements
- Estate planning → CPF nomination should reflect property co-ownership intent
- BTO with 99-year lease → first owner CPF works fine; resale buyer of older flat must check lease vs their age
